Real-Time Anomaly Detection in Stellar Spectra Using Unsupervised Deep Learning

Ninad Naik
Undergraduate Student, Gitam University

Abstract. Modern astronomical surveys generate massive volumes of stellar spectra, making manual inspection of unusual objects impractical. We present an unsupervised, real-time anomaly detection pipeline for stellar spectra that learns normal spectral behavior directly from data and automatically flags unusual candidates without requiring labels. The system is evaluated on real Sloan Digital Sky Survey (SDSS) spectra and demonstrates stable anomaly detection across repeated observation cycles, identifying both physically distinct spectra and data-quality anomalies.

1. Introduction

Large spectroscopic surveys such as the Sloan Digital Sky Survey (SDSS) collect millions of stellar spectra. Identifying rare or unusual objects within these datasets is a key scientific challenge. Traditional approaches rely on predefined classes or manual inspection, which do not scale effectively.

2. Dataset

We use real optical stellar spectra from SDSS, covering wavelengths from approximately 3800 Å to 9200 Å. The data are unlabeled, motivating the use of unsupervised learning methods.

3. Methodology

Each spectrum is resampled onto a common wavelength grid and normalized. A deep autoencoder is trained to learn compact latent representations of stellar spectra. Anomalies are detected using a combination of isolation-based methods applied to the latent space and reconstruction error from the autoencoder.

4. Real-Time Pipeline

The trained model is deployed in a streaming-like setting where spectra are processed sequentially. Anomalies are flagged immediately and logged, simulating the behavior of real astronomical survey pipelines.

6. Discussion

Visual inspection shows that one candidate exhibits a structurally distinct but coherent spectrum, while the other is dominated by noise and calibration artifacts. These results demonstrate the ability of the pipeline to detect both astrophysically interesting objects and data-quality anomalies.

7. Limitations and Future Work

The current study is limited by dataset size and lack of physical confirmation. Future work will scale the pipeline to larger surveys, incorporate Gaia XP spectra, and integrate catalog cross-matching for validation.
